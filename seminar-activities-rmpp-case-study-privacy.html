Unit 4 Seminar: Case Study On Privacy

Ethical issues in deciding which option to pursue

The key ethical issues revolve around privacy, confidentiality, informed consent, and professional responsibility. Ricardo must consider:

  • Whether disclosing names and addresses violates privacy laws or ethical guidelines.
  • If Beth’s request aligns with the intended purpose of the granted access.
  • The potential consequences for individuals whose information may be shared without consent.
  1. Should Ricardo release the names and addresses if he is not responsible for determining allowable access?

    2. No, Ricardo should not release the names and addresses if he is not responsible for determining access. Ethical concerns include:

    • Breach of confidentiality: Ricardo was not given authority to disclose personal information.
    • Violation of policies/laws: Many government records are protected under data protection regulations (such as GDPR or HIPAA, depending on jurisdiction).
    • Abuse of position: Ricardo would be misusing his access to privileged information.

    If Beth needs additional access, she should request permission through the proper channels.

  2. If Ricardo were responsible for determining access, what ethical issues would be involved in his decision?

    3. If Ricardo had the authority to determine access, he would need to consider:

    • Legal constraints: Does the law or local government policy allow the release of personally identifiable information (PII)?
    • Data minimisation: Can Beth conduct the study without needing names and addresses?
    • Harm vs. benefit: Would releasing the data cause potential harm to individuals (e.g., privacy invasion) compared to the benefits of the study?
    • Informed consent: The individuals in the records did not originally consent to be part of Beth’s research.
    • Precedent-setting: Granting access to one researcher may open the door to future unethical requests.
  3. Should Beth be allowed to contact individuals? Ethical issues for the records department

    4. The records department must consider:

    • Risk of harassment: Even if Beth has good intentions, people may feel uncomfortable being contacted.
    • Informed consent & transparency: If the individuals' details are released, they should be informed how their data will be used.
    • Legal obligations: Many jurisdictions have strict privacy laws that prohibit sharing personal data without consent.

    The ethical approach would be for Beth to request an official amendment to her access agreement through the appropriate government channels rather than obtaining data informally.

  4. What options are available to Beth If she does not get enough responses?

    5. If Beth only receives consent from one-third of the individuals, she cannot ethically assume permission from non-respondents. Her options include:

    • Reanalysing the study scope: Adjusting the study to use only the responses received.
    • Seeking alternative data sources: Using public records or anonymised survey data.
    • Applying for additional permissions: Requesting an official exception from the governing authority (if legally allowed).
    • Using statistical methods: Exploring whether extrapolation or weighting techniques can help with data gaps.
    • Ethically increasing outreach: Sending follow-up reminders or using a third-party service to request voluntary participation without coercion.

    The unethical approach would be to assume consent from non-respondents or to pressure Ricardo to provide unauthorised data.

Conclusion

The ethical concerns in this case revolve around privacy, informed consent, professional responsibility, and legal compliance. The best course of action is to ensure that all data access follows official protocols, respects individuals’ privacy, and does not breach ethical or legal guidelines.
