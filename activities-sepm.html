Unit 3: Software Development Life Cycles (SDLC)

  1. Identify and apply appropriate software engineering and project management methodologies, tools and techniques for the development of solutions to real-world problems.
  2. Design, develop and evaluate management systems to produce solutions that handle constraints and uncertainty, contextualising BDD concepts and secure coding principles & practices in SDLC.

RESPONSE: Software Engineering Methodologies

Methodologies Description Use Case
Waterfall Model Is a linear and sequential approach. It divides the process into distinct phases, including requirements analysis, system design, implementation, integration, testing, deployment and maintenance. Best suited for projects with well-defined requirements and little or no expected change.
Agile Model An iterative and incremental approach emphasizing flexibility, customer collaboration, and quick delivery. Includes frameworks such as Scrum and XP (Extreme Programming). Best suited for projects with rapidly changing requirements.
DevOps Model Integrates software development (Dev) and IT operations (Ops) to improve collaboration and automate processes. Components include Continuous Integration (CI), Continuous Deployment (CD), Infrasture as Code (IaC). Best suited for environments requiring continuous delivery and integration.
Spiral Model Combines the repeated testing of prototypes using the Waterfall Model's structure, emphasizing risk analysis and repeated cycles of planning, assessment, engineering and evaluation, each refining software. Best Suited for large, complex, and high-risk projects.
V-Model (Verification and Validation Model) Is an extension of the Waterfall Model that emphasizes verification and validation at each development stage. Best suited for projects where testing is critical, like safety-critical systems.

RESPONSE: Project Management Methodologies

Methodologies Description Use Case
PMBOK (Project Management Body of Knowledge) A comprehensive set of guidelines and best practices defined by the Project Management Institute (PMI) with knowledge areas such as Scope, Time, Cost, Quality, Resource, Communication, Risk Procurement and Stakeholder Management. Used in various industries for structured project management.
PRINCE2 (Projects In Controlled Environments A process-driven project management methodology that provides a clearly defined framework using principles for Business Justification, Learning from Experience or Managing by Stages. Best suited of commonly used in government projects, particularly in the UK and Europe.
Scrum An Agile Framework focusing on repeated progress through cycles designed to help teams work together more effectively by breaking down projects into small pieces allowing for continuous feedback. Suited for dynamic projects with frequent changes using tools like Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, and Increment tracked in Trello, monday.com or ClickUp.
Lean Focuses on maximising value by eliminating waste and optimising processes. It uses principles such as Value, Value Stream, Flow, Pull and Perfection. Suited for projects with a focus on efficiency and resource optimisation.

RESPONSE: Design, develop and evaluate management systems

Design, develop and evaluate management systems to produce solutions that handle constraints and uncertainty, contextualising BDD concepts and secure coding principles & practices in SDLC.

The task of designing, developing and evaluating management systems that produce solutions that can handle constraints and uncertainty in the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) involves a much deeper understanding of project management principles. This includes understanding software development and coding practices to ensure that the software produced functions and is secure. It means applying a robust Behaviour-Driven Development within an environment. Although this might be complex it’s doable.

Design Phases

The design of this kind of management system that will be able to handle constraints, and uncertainty starts with an understanding of the entire project scope including requirements. This phase is critical as it’s setting the foundation for evolving requirements and how to approach constraints such as

  • Limited time
  • Budget and
  • Resources

This is a key element in developing a robust risk management plan, which identifies potential risks early in the project and devises strategies to mitigate them (Stellman & Greene, 2008).

Behaviour-Driven Management(BDD) at this phase is also essential

  • It ensures that all stakeholders are involved early in the process
  • It verifies that the requirements are clearly understood and agreed upon
  • It can confirm understanding by using natural language descriptions to help reduce any ambiguity
  • It guarantees that the team share an understanding of the goals

This shared understanding is crucial for managing constraints and uncertainty, ensuring everyone is aligned.

Development Phase

During development the design management system is implemented, with BDD and secure coding practices playing pivotal roles. BDD emphasizes collaboration between developers, testers, and non-technical stakeholders using examples to guide development. This approach helps in continuously validating that the software meets the desired requirements, thus managing uncertainty and ensuring that constraints are respected (University of Essex, 2024, 06:30).

Secure coding principles should be integrated throughout the SDLC to prevent vulnerabilities from being introduced into the system. This involves adopting coding practices that are aligned with the industry standards, conducting code reviews, and using automated tools to detect security flaws early in the development process. The integration of secure coding into the management system ensures that security is not an afterthought but a continuous focus throughout the project (Stellman & Greene, 2008).

Evaluation Phase

Evaluating the management system involves assessing how well it handles the identified constraints and uncertainty. This can be done through regular reviews and testing, ensuring that the system adapts to any changes in requirements or project scope. The effectiveness of BDD can be evaluated by examining how well it facilitated communication between stakeholders and how it contributed to the early detection of issues. Similarly, the success of secure coding practices can be assessed by the absence of security vulnerabilities in the final product (University of Essex, 2024, 06:30).

In conclusion, designing, developing, and evaluating management systems within the SDLC requires a careful balance of project management practices, collaborative development approaches like BDD, and rigorous application of secure coding principles. By integrating these elements, you can create a system that not only meets its functional requirements but does so within the constraints of time, budget, and resources while effectively managing the uncertainties inherent in software development.
