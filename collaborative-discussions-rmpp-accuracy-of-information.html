Case Study: Accuracy of information

Research Methods and Professional Practice

Contribution by Zukiswa Tuso

Abi, a UK-based researcher, faces an ethical dilemma when data refute claims about a new cereal, Whizzz. Selectively analysing correct data to support a positive conclusion would be unethical, breaching the BCS Code of Conduct (BCS, 2022) and undermining research integrity (UKRIO, 2019). Ethical practice demands presenting both positive and negative findings (Joober et al., 2012) to avoid misleading stakeholders and contributing to publication bias (Fanelli, 2012).

Abi is obligated to ensure the accuracy of his reporting, as consumer protection laws (CPUTR, 2008) prohibit misleading claims. Under the UK GDPR (2018), he must also handle any personal data lawfully. If the manufacturer uses his results selectively, Abi remains partially responsible and should mitigate misuse by communicating findings clearly (ASA, 2022). If pressured to conceal unfavourable results, whistleblowing under the Public Interest Disclosure Act (1998) is a legal safeguard.

Socially, misleading reporting could erode public trust in science. Culturally, upholding honesty aligns with UK values of fair practice and scientific transparency. Abi’s best course is to present a full, balanced report, supporting both professional integrity and public interest.
